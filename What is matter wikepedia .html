Bose-Einstein Condensate

A Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC) is a fifth state of matter formed when bosons (e.g., rubidium atoms) are cooled to temperatures near absolute zero (-273.15). At this ultra-cold limit, individual atoms lose their identity and merge into a single, collective "super-atom" wave, exhibiting quantum phenomena on a macroscopic scale

Super solid

A supersolid forms when quantum particles (typically atoms or light-matter particles called polaritons) are cooled to temperatures near absolute zero, causing them to simultaneously condense into an ordered, rigid crystal structure and act as a superfluid with zero viscosity .A supersolid is an exotic, quantum state of matter that simultaneously behaves as a rigid, ordered crystal and a frictionless, superfluid liquid.It combines crystalline structure (fixed, periodic atom arrangement) with superfluidity (atoms moving without energy loss or friction).Unlike normal solids, a supersolid can change its density and flow; unlike normal liquids, it maintains its crystalline internal structure.It is a manifestation of quantum mechanics where particles exist in a highly correlated state at extremely low temperatures.

Super fluid

A superfluid is a state of matter—typically liquid helium cooled to near absolute zero (-273.15)—characterized by zero viscosity and zero friction. It flows without losing energy, can climb up container walls, and passes through microscopic pores, enabling it to leak out of sealed containers. Unlike normal fluids, superfluids have no internal friction, allowing them to flow indefinitely without losing kinetic energy.
